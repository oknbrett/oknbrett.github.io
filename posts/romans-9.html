Romans 9 and the
God Who Chooses

Brett  ·  April 2026  ·  8 min read

Romans 9 is the chapter people argue about. It is the chapter that made me Reformed, and the chapter I have seen people walk away from — not because it is unclear, but because it is too clear.

Paul does not bury the lead. He opens with grief and ends with doxology. What happens in between is one of the most careful, most honest pieces of theological reasoning in all of Scripture.

The Anguish That Opens Everything

Before Paul says anything about election, he cries. "I have great sorrow and unceasing anguish in my heart." He says he could wish himself accursed — cut off from Christ — for the sake of his kinsmen. That is not rhetoric. That is the posture of a man who knows exactly what is at stake in what he is about to argue.

I am speaking the truth in Christ — I am not lying; my conscience bears me witness in the Holy Spirit — that I have great sorrow and unceasing anguish in my heart. For I could wish that I myself were accursed and cut off from Christ for the sake of my brothers, my kinsmen according to the flesh. Romans 9:1–3, ESV

The doctrine of election is never meant to be cold. Paul holds it with tears. That should matter to us.

The Thesis: God's Word Has Not Failed

Verse 6 is the hinge. Paul has just listed everything Israel received — adoption, glory, covenants, the law, the worship, the promises, the patriarchs, and the Messiah himself. And then Israel, by and large, rejected that Messiah. Does that mean God's promises failed?

But it is not as though the word of God has failed. For not all who are descended from Israel belong to Israel. Romans 9:6, ESV

This is the move. Paul redefines the "Israel" to whom the promises apply. The promises were never to ethnic Israel as a whole. There has always been an election within the election — a true Israel inside national Israel.

Two Case Studies in Unconditional Election

Abraham's children

Not all Abraham's biological children are "the seed." Isaac, not Ishmael. The line runs through promise, not biology. Paul is very specific: "it is not the children of the flesh who are the children of God, but the children of the promise are counted as offspring." The promise precedes and creates the people, not the other way around.

Jacob and Esau

This is where the argument sharpens to a point. Same father, same mother, same womb, same conception. Before they were born, before either had done anything good or bad, God chose Jacob and passed over Esau. Paul quotes Malachi: "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated."

"Not because of works but because of him who calls."

The phrase doing the most theological work here is verse 12: "not because of works but because of him who calls." Election is grounded entirely in the sovereign will of the one calling, not in any foreseen merit, faith, or response in the one called. That is the plain meaning of the text.

Is God Unjust?

Paul knows this sounds like a problem. So he raises the objection himself: "Is there injustice on God's part?" And he answers with something that often disappoints people looking for a philosophical resolution.

He quotes Exodus 33:19. "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion." God's freedom to show mercy is not constrained by human desert. And then verse 16, as clean and final as anything in Paul: "So then it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy."

He does not say the question is unfair. He does not soften the doctrine. He answers by pointing to God's sovereign freedom and leaves it there. That is uncomfortable, and Paul knows it. The discomfort is the point.

The Potter and the Clay

The second objection in verse 19 is sharper: "Why does he still find fault? For who can resist his will?" If God sovereignly hardens, why is anyone blamed?

Paul's answer is not a syllogism. It is a posture.

But who are you, O man, to answer back to God? Will what is molded say to its molder, "Why have you made me like this?" Has the potter no right over the clay, to make out of the same lump one vessel for honorable use and another for dishonorable use? Romans 9:20–21, ESV

This is not intellectual bullying. This is the creatureliness of the creature brought into full view. The question "why" assumes a vantage point we do not have. We are clay. The potter is not obligated to justify himself to the pot.

Verses 22–23 then show us the purpose behind the asymmetry: God endures vessels of wrath "in order to make known the riches of his glory for vessels of mercy." The ultimate end is not God's dominance for its own sake. It is the display of his glory in mercy.

What This Chapter Is Actually About

Romans 9 is not a chapter about abstract double predestination. It is a chapter about the reliability of God's word. Paul's question is pastoral: can we trust the promises? His answer is yes — not because we can always explain God's ways, but because God is sovereign over his own purposes. The elect will be saved. Not because of our faithfulness, but because of his.

The God who chose before the foundation of the world is the same God who calls you today.

I came to this chapter young. I read it and something settled in me that has not moved since. Not a sense of pride — election is the death of that. But a deep, strange peace. If it depended on my will or my exertion, I would be finished. It depends on God who has mercy.

Prone to wander. Yes. But held.
